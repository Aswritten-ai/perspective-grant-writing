# Computational Biology Proposals: Domain-Specific Patterns

**Date:** 2025-06-12
**Source:** Dr. James Thornton, computational biologist, funded 4x by NSF CAREER and R01
**Context:** Mentoring session with early-career faculty at Cold Spring Harbor

## The comp bio disadvantage

Computational proposals face a structural bias: reviewers from wet-lab backgrounds don't always see algorithm development as "real" biology. The trick is to frame every computational contribution in terms of biological questions it answers.

> "I never describe my methods as 'novel algorithms.' I describe them as 'approaches that let us ask questions we couldn't ask before.' The algorithm is how — the biology is why. Reviewers fund the why."

## Preliminary data strategy

For computational work, preliminary data doesn't mean running your pipeline on toy data. It means showing that your approach reveals something biologically unexpected in real data — even if the analysis is incomplete.

> "Show one figure where your method finds something a standard approach misses. That's worth more than ten benchmarks on simulated data."

## The reproducibility argument

Computational proposals have a built-in broader impact: reproducibility. Every pipeline, every workflow, every documented parameter choice is a contribution to open science. But you have to frame it as a community resource, not just "we'll put the code on GitHub."
