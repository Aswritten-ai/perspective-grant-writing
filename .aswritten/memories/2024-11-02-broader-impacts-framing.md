# Broader Impacts: Beyond the Checkbox

**Date:** 2024-11-02
**Source:** Dr. Marcus Williams, program officer (retired), NSF Division of Biological Infrastructure
**Context:** Phone interview about common broader impacts mistakes

## The broader impacts problem

Most PIs treat broader impacts as a tax — something you bolt on to satisfy the requirement. Reviewers can tell instantly. The best proposals integrate broader impacts into the research design itself, so the impact isn't separate from the science.

> "When I see 'we will mentor two undergraduates,' I know the PI hasn't thought about this. When I see 'our method requires field validation in underserved watersheds, which creates natural partnerships with tribal colleges who monitor those same systems' — that's a broader impact that's also better science."

## What actually scores well

- **Impacts that improve the research** — community partnerships that provide access, training programs that generate data, outreach that validates assumptions
- **Specificity over ambition** — "we will partner with Diné College's water monitoring program" beats "we will engage underrepresented communities"
- **Evidence of existing relationships** — a letter of support from a community partner you've already worked with is worth more than five paragraphs of plans

## The institutional trap

Universities push PIs toward their existing outreach infrastructure — "use our REU program, mention our STEM pipeline." This produces identical broader impacts sections across every proposal from that institution. Reviewers notice.

> "If I read three proposals from the same university and they all mention the same summer program, I know none of those PIs actually thought about broader impacts. They copy-pasted from the last funded proposal in their department."
