# Budget Justification: The Hidden Scoring Criterion

**Date:** 2025-01-20
**Source:** Dr. Rachel Okafor, grants administrator, Johns Hopkins University
**Context:** Internal training session for junior faculty on "budget red flags"

## Budget tells the story of your project

The budget justification is the only part of the proposal where reviewers can see whether the PI actually knows how to execute. A vague budget signals a vague plan.

> "I've seen brilliant science get triaged because the budget asked for a postdoc at 100% effort but the timeline showed three parallel workstreams. The reviewer does the math. If your budget doesn't add up, your project plan doesn't either."

## Patterns that work

- **Effort allocations that match milestones** — if Year 2 is the field season, that's where the travel and personnel costs concentrate
- **Equipment justified by capability gap** — not "we need a mass spec" but "the nearest accessible instrument is 200 miles away and our samples degrade within 4 hours"
- **Consultant costs that signal expertise gaps you're honest about** — reviewers respect PIs who know what they don't know

## Common mistakes

- Requesting summer salary without explaining what changes during the academic year
- Travel budgets that don't connect to specific collaborations or field sites
- Lumping supplies into a single line item (signals you haven't planned the experiments)

> "The PIs who get funded consistently are the ones whose budgets read like a project plan. Every dollar has a reason, and the reasons connect to the narrative in the project description."
