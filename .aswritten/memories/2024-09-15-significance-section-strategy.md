# Grant Writing: The Significance Section

**Date:** 2024-09-15
**Source:** Dr. Sarah Chen, former NSF review panel member
**Context:** Workshop on "What reviewers actually look for" at the Grant Writers' Collective annual conference

## What reviewers actually look for

The significance section is where most proposals fail — not because the science is weak, but because the PI doesn't connect their work to the reviewer's frame of reference. Reviewers read 30-40 proposals in a session. They're looking for the one that makes them say "oh, this matters because..."

The trick is: don't tell the reviewer why it's important. Show them a world where the problem is solved and ask them to imagine the difference.

> "I tell every PI the same thing: your significance section isn't about your research. It's about the reviewer's understanding of the field. You're not arguing that your work matters — you're showing them a gap in their mental model they didn't know existed."

The three things that make a significance section land:

1. **The gap statement** — what's missing from the current understanding, framed as a tension the reviewer already feels
2. **The consequence frame** — what happens if this gap persists (not "this is important" but "here's what we can't do without this")
3. **The unlock** — what becomes possible once this gap is closed, stated in terms the reviewer's own work connects to

## What doesn't work

- Lists of statistics about the problem's prevalence
- Claims that "no one has studied X" (reviewers read this as "maybe there's a reason")
- Significance sections that are really just background sections with the heading changed
