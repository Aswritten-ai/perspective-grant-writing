# How Reviewers Actually Read Proposals

**Date:** 2025-03-08
**Source:** Dr. Sarah Chen + panel discussion with 4 current/former NSF reviewers
**Context:** Grant Writers' Collective webinar, "Inside the review room"

## The first 90 seconds

Every reviewer develops a gut reaction in the first 90 seconds of reading. The rest of the review is either confirming or fighting that reaction. This means your specific aims page is the most important page in the proposal.

> "I'll be honest — by the time I finish the specific aims, I've already decided if this is a fund or a decline. Everything after that either confirms my instinct or occasionally changes my mind. But changing my mind takes work, and I'm reading 35 of these."

## The scoring heuristic

Reviewers don't score each section independently and average. They have an overall impression and then find evidence to support it. This means:

- A strong aims page with a mediocre methods section still gets funded
- A mediocre aims page with brilliant methods rarely recovers
- One memorable phrase or framing can carry the entire review

> "I can only fight for three proposals in the panel discussion. I fight for the ones I can explain in one sentence to the other panelists. If your proposal doesn't give me that sentence, I can't advocate for you."

## What triggers a "triage" (not discussed)

- Aims that are really tasks, not questions
- Significance that's really background
- Innovation claims that cite the PI's own prior work as the field gap
- Budgets that suggest the PI has never actually run the proposed experiments
